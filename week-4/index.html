# Oct 10th Update ## Calculations Microwaving a steak takes 5-7 minutes, and the mircowave emits 1kW of power, so we get between 300 and 420 kW seconds, translating to .3 MJ to .42 MJ to microwave the steak. I used these real world numbers as they account for some heat losses and inefficiency, although I am not sure if direct mircowave radiation may be more efficient than conduction. Additionally, the microwave contains most of the heat since it is a closed container. This math was done based on [***this***](https://www.businessinsider.com/yes-you-can-microwave-a-steak-heres-how-2016-6) Business Insider article describing the microwaving of a one inch thick steak. The amount of energy needed to heat one liter of water from 20 to 100 degrees Celsius is .183 kWh or .659 MJ. Thus boiling a liter of water and preparing a steak requires about 1 MJ of energy. Heating up tomatoes requires almost exactly the same amount of energy as water since they are 95% water, and that yields a high estimate as water has the highest specific heat of any liquid. Assuming we want to cook the steak, as decribed in the Business Insider article, boil a liter of water, and then prepare a kilogram of tomato and rice (combined kilogram), we expect to need about 2MJ of energy worth of heat. Assuming approximately 30% efficiency, we need about 6MJ of total energy stored. The first PDF attached to the design section shows my calculations for the amounts space and mass needed to store the energy necessary to safely store enough energy for the cooking described in the above paragraph. Using CaO --> Ca(OH)2 we need 1.5 liters (1.5kg) of water and 1.25L (4kg) of CaO, giving at least 30% overhead on the material requirements. This corresponds to 3 mm of space needed underneath the cooking surface to store the CaO and water. The cost of this much CaO is about ***$30***. Perhaps a safer alternative is to use Erythritol (C4H10O4) as a means of phase change storage. This would require 25kg, or about 17L of C4H10O4, with a similar margin to that of the CaO. Accounting for maximum thermal expansion of about 16%, we get that 2.25cm of space are needed below the cooking surface to store the Erythritol.
