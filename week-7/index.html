# Nov 7 Update - Mid-Review Update and Recap ### References: Solar Path Polar Coordinates [***here***](https://www.gaisma.com/en/location/boston-massachusetts.html) Solar Path Rectangular Coordinates [***here***](http://solardat.uoregon.edu/SunChartProgram.html) ## Mid Review Recap ### Collectors We reviewed and presented a number of ways in which we can concentrate solar energy. The most simple method would likely be to purchase a fresnel lense. The advantage of a Fresnel lens is that we would not have to do much besides build a frame to hold it steady and the fact that the focal point is on the opposit of the light source means that we need no holes or mirrors to direct the sunlight into the tube that brings down sunlight. Another benefit of the Fresnel lens is that it would serve as an umbrella for the tube, preventing rain from getting in. The Fresnel lens does have two downsides: that it has a very textured surface and may be hard to clean as a result, and that it has a low maximum efficiency of around 82%. Next in terms of ease of manufacture is the collector made out of many small mirrors, each attached to a backing board at the right angle to gather all the light into the focal point. The advantage of this method is that most of manufacturing would be done by a CNC cutting into foam or wood, and would only require us to program the angles of each small part of the surface and then for us to carefully glue each mirror on to the surface. A slight variation of this would be to cut out parts of a parabola with a laser cutter so that the edges line up. For this we would probably glue mylar onto the cut out pieces of acrylic. Producing a spherical approximation of a parabolic mirror would likely be a fast, albeit complicated way of making the collector. We would begin by cutting a hole in a piece of smooth PVC board that is about 36"x36" and then marking a circle with an 18" radius around the center hole. In the central hole we would glue in a Schrader bike valve and then connect it to a bike pump. Next, we would put epoxy along the outside of the circle that we marked on the board, and would then draw a piece of emergecy blanket over the circle of epoxy, to seal it to the board. Once the epoxy cures, we could then pressurize the mylar with the bike pump, and could then proceed to lay on 3-5 layers of fiberglass (or potentially [***burlap***](http://fab.cba.mit.edu/classes/863.14/people/tim_fallon/project-09.html)) on the back of the now curved mylar, adding a circular reinforcement around the rim to ensure rigidity. Both the modular and one piece mirror collectors would require a second, higher quality reflector, as pointed out during out the Mid-Review, and for this a commercial convex mirror could be modified by removing the protective paint on the back with airplane paint remover. Our audience for the Mid-Review was also helpful in pointing out to us that we should incorporate a fine adjuster for the secondary reflector since our first surface may not be aligned exactly as we intend it to be. One idea resuscitated by our audience was that of the parabolic trough, particularly its benefits as it only needs to track along one axis. Were it not for the problem of moving all the light down to the battery, this would have been one of the most promising designs as it requires simple curves, rather than compound curves. ### Tracking Although we did not recieve many comments on tracking, besides the fact that it may be possible to track the sun manually if one does not desire to leave the collector unattended for safety reasons. We exhibited the possibility of using NOAA data to guide the turret that will aim the mirror at the sun with no inputs. The other possibilities we presented were using photo-resistors or solar panels to guide the motors, directly or through a microprocessor. The benefit of photo-resistors is that the code has already been written, the benefit of solar panels is that it would diminish the requirement for drawing power from a battery. Both methods would need some sort of external controller or clever engineering to have them adjust themselves towards the sun every morning. ### General Integration Dealing with rain is the biggest integration problem that we had not yet considered. We had considered wind, since it is not uncommon for the sun to shine while the wind blows. While we found that diminishing the size of the collector could solve the problem of wind, it seems that we will likely need to add a clear piece of glass to the center of the collecting dish to keep out water. Furthermore, we will need to come up with a way of preventing condensation from building up in the mirrored tube, as this would ruin its efficacy. A drain at the bottom of the mirrored tube would likely help water from accumulating over time. Another approach is to design the mirrored tube so that manageable parts can be removed and cleaned with relative ease, which would not be the case for removing the whole tube at once.
